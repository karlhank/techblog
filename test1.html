Are you experiencing windows popping up on your PC claiming you have a virus infection?

You can't access any web sites or do anything with your browser because of "virus" warnings?

There is a threat to the PCs in your office and in your home. This threat is from fake security software called Scareware or Ransomware.

What is Scareware?
A scareware attack is a legitimate looking security application that “pops” up as you are browsing the web. These applications go by many, many names such as Antivirus 2009, Antivirus 2010, Antivirus Agent Pro, Advanced Virus Remover and Antivirus+ or Security Tool. The makers of these applications prey upon unsuspecting users with unethical marketing practices designed to cause shock, anxiety or the perception of a threat.

How do you recognize a scareware attack?
Scareware attacks usually have several common behaviors. They will pretend to be scanning your machine, often with an animated progress bar. They will usually say that your machine is infected with one or more viruses or malicious software, or malware. These scans are fake and they have no way to access your hard drive. They often show logos of reputable sites they say recommend their software. They may also have graphs and charts expounding the ability their software has in cleaning your PC. They usually look very legitimate. Some will show realistic Microsoft Windows screens. Click here to see some examples of scareware windows. It is important that when you see one of these windows that you do not click on any part of the window. Often the criminals who make these screens make them one giant button that if clicked will allow malicious software to be installed on your machine.

Where do the attacks come from?
Cybercriminals are always looking for new ways to spread their malware. Hijacked search terms pointing to illegitimate websites is a popular method. The attackers use popular search terms to point users to their websites. Another method is malicious advertising, or malvertising. Malvertising is the practice of serving malicious ads on legitimate websites. Your PC can get infected by simply browsing to a legitimate website. These websites do their best to limit these ads but it can be a challenge.

What can we do to protect ourselves?
· Prevention is the best medicine. The best way that you can protect yourselves is to pay attention to the links you are clicking on. If it does not seem legitimate it probably is not.

· Be careful of attachments and links in emails. If you were not expecting the email it is better to ask the sender if they sent you the email than to risk a virus or malware infection. I look for something personalized from the sender such as a how-are-you note or something else that tells you it was sent by the sender and not by malicious software on their machine.

· Be careful of links to foreign countries. Links that have a .cn or .ru instead of .com.

· Of course even the most careful person will eventually be presented with one of these scareware windows. When a scareware window “pops” up do not click on any part of the window. Even the red “X” in the corner. The best way to close these windows is to use Alt-F4. Press and hold down the Alt key and then press F4 and current window will close. You might need to press F4 multiple times to get all the scareware windows to close. The other method to close these windows is to right click on the windows taskbar icon at the bottom of your monitor and select close. One of these method should allow you to safely close the malicious windows.

· If for whatever reason you can’t get the scareware windows to close, then turn off your PC. Just hold the power button down until the PC shuts down. Don’t try to exit Windows.
